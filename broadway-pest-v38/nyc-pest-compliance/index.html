HPD Pest Violations: Classes, Deadlines & How to Clear Them

New York City’s Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) agency enforces housing maintenance standards under the Housing Maintenance Code (HMC). When a tenant complaint or HPD inspection identifies a pest condition in a residential building, HPD issues a formal violation that the property owner must correct and certify.

HPD Violation Classes

HPD classifies pest violations into three categories based on hazard level:

ClassDesignationCorrection DeadlineExamples
Class CImmediately Hazardous24 hoursRodents, cockroach infestation in kitchen, bed bugs with children present
Class BHazardous30 daysMouse evidence, cockroach activity in non-food areas
Class ANon-Hazardous90 daysIsolated pest sightings with minimal evidence

Important: If HPD issues a Class C violation and the owner does not correct it within 24 hours, HPD may hire emergency contractors to perform the work and bill the owner — often at rates far exceeding what a pest control company would charge.

How to Certify Correction of an HPD Pest Violation

After treatment, property owners must certify correction through the HPD Online portal (hpdonline.nyc.gov). Certification requires:

  • Date of treatment
  • Name and license number of the pest control operator
  • Description of treatment performed
  • Pest type addressed
  • Corrective measures taken to prevent re-infestation (exclusion, sanitation recommendations)

NYC DOH Restaurant Inspections: How Pest Violations Affect Your Grade

The NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (DOHMH) conducts unannounced inspections of all NYC food service establishments. The grading system assigns points for each violation observed, with grades assigned based on total point accumulation.

DOH Grading Thresholds

GradePointsImpact
A0 – 13 pointsGrade A posted; no re-inspection required
B14 – 27 pointsGrade Pending posted; re-inspection scheduled within 1 month
C28+ pointsGrade Pending posted; adjudication hearing required

Pest-Related DOH Violation Points

Pest conditions are among the highest-point violations in the DOHMH inspection rubric:

  • Mice or live rats in the establishment — 28 points (Critical)
  • Cockroaches or live roaches in food/prep areas — 28 points (Critical)
  • Evidence of mice (droppings, gnawing) — 3–7 points
  • Evidence of cockroaches (egg cases, cast skins) — 3 points
  • Pest control program absent or inadequate — 2 points

A single live rodent sighting during a DOH inspection can generate enough points to drop an A-grade establishment to a B or C in one visit. The most effective defense is maintaining active, documented pest control service before inspections occur — not reacting after.

Pro tip from 55 years in NYC: DOH inspectors check pest control service records as part of every inspection. Establishments with a documented, continuous IPM program receive more favorable consideration than those without records on file.

Local Law 55: NYC’s IPM Mandate for Buildings

Local Law 55 of 2018 amended the NYC Administrative Code to require Integrated Pest Management (IPM) practices in residential buildings. The law reflects a shift from reactive pesticide application to prevention-first pest management.

What Local Law 55 Requires

  • Building owners must respond to pest complaints within 30 days (Class B) or immediately for Class C conditions
  • All pest management must follow IPM principles: inspection, identification, prevention, and monitoring before pesticide use
  • Only licensed pesticide applicators may apply pesticides in residential buildings
  • Owners must maintain written records of all pest conditions reported and treatments performed
  • Pesticide applications must be documented including product name, EPA registration number, application location, and date

IPM Under Local Law 55: The Four-Step Framework

Effective IPM under Local Law 55 follows a structured process:

  • Inspection: Systematic examination to identify pest species, entry points, harborage areas, and contributing conditions
  • Prevention & Exclusion: Sealing cracks, gaps, and pipe penetrations; correcting moisture issues; improving sanitation
  • Monitoring: Ongoing trapping and inspection to assess pest pressure
  • Treatment: Targeted pesticide application only when monitoring indicates threshold levels have been reached

HACCP & FSMA: Food Safety Documentation Requirements

For food service and food processing facilities, pest control documentation intersects with federal food safety law under two frameworks: Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) and the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA).

HACCP Pest Control Requirements

HACCP is the food safety framework used by restaurants, food processors, and food distributors. Pest control is a prerequisite program under HACCP — meaning it must be in place and documented before HACCP plans are even implemented. HACCP auditors expect to see:

  • Active pest control service contract with a licensed operator
  • Written service reports for every visit, including findings and treatments
  • Evidence of follow-up on recommendations (exclusion work, sanitation corrections)
  • Pesticide product labels and Safety Data Sheets (SDS) on file
  • Pest sighting logs maintained by facility staff

FSMA Pest Control Requirements

The FDA’s Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) Preventive Controls rules require food facilities to implement and document environmental monitoring programs. Pest control is explicitly included as a supply chain program element. Key FSMA documentation requirements include:

  • Written pest management procedures and pest control program records
  • Corrective actions taken in response to pest findings
  • Verification records confirming pest control activities were completed as planned
  • Supplier verification for pest control contractors (license, insurance, QualityPro certification)

Hotels & Short-Term Rentals: Pest Compliance in NYC Hospitality

NYC hotels operate under a combination of DOH oversight (food service areas), HPD-equivalent standards for guestroom habitability, and brand/franchisor Quality Assurance (QA) requirements. Pest findings during brand QA audits can result in warning letters, remediation plans, or in severe cases, termination of brand agreements.

Key Hospitality Compliance Points

  • Bed bug response protocols must be documented and trainable by front-line staff
  • Brand QA auditors increasingly request pest control service records as standard audit items
  • NYC hotels are subject to HPD-equivalent standards for guest rooms under the Multiple Dwelling Law
  • Restaurant and banquet facilities within hotels are subject to full DOH inspection and grading
  • Documenting pest-free status protects against tenant/guest litigation
